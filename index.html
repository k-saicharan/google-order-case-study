The Simulation

Take the seat. Six forks, one held parcel.

No special access and no hindsight, only the console an operator is given and the authority that comes with it. At each fork the correct move is one a competent person in the chair can see. Watch the point where the chair runs out of authority.

The rules of the seat

Every "I did not receive it" is sorted at intake into one of two modes. Remediate: treat it as a fulfilment failure the company owns, and fix it. Adjudicate: treat it as an exception the customer is requesting, and decide whether to grant it; the institutional default here is deny until proven. High-value, in-limbo cases fall into Adjudicate by default.

There are also two structurally different "not received" claims. Class A: the carrier's record shows delivered and the customer disputes it; this is the only place fraud can live. Class B: the carrier's own record shows held, never delivered, never scanned out; here the carrier's record rules fraud out entirely. This case is unambiguously Class B. The carrier's last-scan disposition is the fact that should route it. It is never the fact that routes it.

📥
Fork 1 · Intake
A failure to fix, or a claim to assess?
A customer reports an order that never arrived and shows no tracking.

The right call. The carrier shows the parcel held, so this is Class B: a fulfilment failure the company owns. Log it to Remediate and chase the carrier yourself.

The wall. It enters as a support enquiry, which is Adjudicate mode by default. The mode is set wrong at the door, before anyone reads the carrier record.

Outcome
Everything downstream now runs on "deny until proven," applied to a case that had nothing to prove.
📦
Fork 2 · Blind to the carrier
Read the carrier, or wait to be told?
The operator's console shows no live carrier status while the parcel sits on a dangerous-goods hold.

The right call. Pull the carrier's own tracking. Its last scan says held on a DG flag, not lost. Treat that as the fact that decides the case.

The wall. The seat has no live carrier view, so the dispositive fact only enters the case when the customer sends the company a screenshot of the carrier's own page.

Outcome
The one fact that should route the case arrives as a customer attachment, days late, into a case already mis-moded. See the record.
⚖
Fork 3 · The value split
Two items on one order. Clear both?
Same call, same agent: a cheap accessory and a high-value phone.

The right call. Both are the same failure. Resolve both from the chair.

The wall. The accessory clears on the spot; the phone routes to a specialist team. Authority is sliced by value and risk, not by the problem.

Outcome
The accessory is delivered. The phone leaves the only seat that was actually talking to the customer. See the two orders.
⛔
Fork 4 · Inside the specialist queue
Reship the held phone, or not?
High-value phone, held but not confirmed lost, with a trade-in and a promo bound to the order.

The right call. Terminalise the held unit first: release, return-to-sender, or dispose. That retires the double-fulfilment risk and clears the path to a clean reship.

The wall. The seat has no interface to carrier operations, so it cannot terminalise anything. The only lever it holds is grant or deny, and reshipping an unconfirmed-lost asset risks paying twice.

Outcome
The denial is born here. Class B rules out any doubt about the customer; what remains is double-fulfilment risk, met with the only tool the seat holds.
⌛
Fork 5 · The limbo has no clock
The unit is stuck between two teams. Who moves it?
The held parcel sits between carrier operations and support; neither can act without the other.

The right call. Escalate on elapsed time. A held asset past a set number of days must force a terminal decision.

The wall. Carrier ops waits on the shipper; the support seat waits on carrier ops; remediation cannot proceed while the unit is in limbo. The DG flag makes terminal resolution compliance-heavy. And nothing measures elapsed limbo, so nothing escalates it.

Outcome · the payload
This is the whole answer to why it took 32 days. A body at rest with no internal force. Every stall window ran out with no owner and no clock. See the tally.
🔁
Fork 6 · The denial, three times
Pressed for specifics: reclassify, or re-run the review?
The customer asks plainly what was denied and why, and offers an explicit way out.

The right call. Reclassify as a fulfilment failure, which dissolves the exception question entirely.

The wall. There is no control to reclassify, and the metrics reward closing the ticket. So the seat re-runs the exception review with the same missing authority and emits the same paragraph. The customer's question, is this a real decision or is it just stuck, cannot be answered honestly from the chair.

Outcome
The same paragraph, three times, no clause, no answer. See the denials.

"The only things that ever moved the case came from outside the company: a payment pause filed with Klarna, a post made in public. That is the proof there was no internal force at all."

The Finding

Alignment holds below a threshold, and inverts above it.

The case was fixable at every fork, and a competent person in the seat could see the fix. What changed with the value of the item was whether the seat was allowed to make it.

"A body at rest with no internal force. Everything that ever moved it came from outside."

The seat, not the person, is where this failed.

Below a certain value, and inside Remediate mode, the company and the customer want the same thing, and a competent operator simply delivers it. That is why the accessory was fixed in a single call.

Above that value and risk threshold, the alignment inverts. Satisfying the customer becomes the operator's locally risky move, because reshipping an asset that is held but not confirmed lost risks paying twice. So the same competent, well-meaning operator denies. The cause is authority the seat was never given.

The 32 days are not a slow queue. They are a limbo asset with no owner and no clock. The held unit can only reach a terminal state through carrier operations; the customer-facing seat has no way to reach carrier operations; remediation cannot proceed while the unit is in limbo; and because nothing measures elapsed limbo, nothing escalates. The only forces that ever moved it came from outside the company: a Klarna dispute, a public post.

At every fork the correct move existed, and a competent person in the seat could see it. The organisation withheld the authority to make it. That is how a case fixable on day 7 compounds into day 32.
